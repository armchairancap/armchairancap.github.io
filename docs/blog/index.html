Introduction​

StarKey wallet is the native wallet for Supra L1.

xx coin is the native token of xx Network, a L1 network with integrated mixnet services for post-quantum privacy.

WXX is an XX Foundation-issued, Ethereum-wrapped ERC20 token. It is also used on Uniswap DEX.

This post explores StarKey for xx coin users (and speculators).

Non-native tokens/coins/NFTs in StarKey​

Supra StarKey is expanding its token support and currently support for 20 or so is natively built-in. What about the smalle ones, such as xx coin?

According to StarKey FAQs:

As of now, StarKey supports a variety of tokens, including all ERC-20 tokens, as well as tokens on the Aptos, Sui, and Solana blockchains

This looks promising. Let's try!

WXX contract​

Go to contract address and copy contract (Token Contract (WITH 9 Decimals) on right-hand side).

I got it from https://etherscan.io/token/0x171120219d3223E008558654ec3254A0F206edb2, but I suggest you get it from Uniswap by yourself.

Get StarKey​

Download StarKey by following links from the official site (www.supra.com) or the StarKey app page.

Currently desktop versions have more features, but I can't think of any that would interfere with WXX.

I used StarKey on Chromium.

Add WXX to StarKey​

warning

Make a backup of your StarKey pass phrase. Like with other non-custodial walles, there's no other way to get access.

The pencil icon is where you toggle display (ON/OFF) of existing tokens. But WXX does not exist, so we need to add it.

Once in Manage Token page, you can toggle other tokens (Solana, etc.), but to add WXX you need to click on the + icon to add a custom token.

This is where the WXX contract is needed:

  • Network: Ethereum
  • Contract: paste the WXX contract address you got earlier

The rest will be auto-populated.

You have added WXX!

Now you can go back to Manage Tokens (the pencil icon). Use the magnifying glass icon to find WXX and click the star to make the token a favorite.

Finally, we can click on WXX to receive or send (ETH is required for transaction fees) WXX.

tip

I encountered a problem with StarKey v2.1.2 in this last step: no coins - built-in or manually added - with zero balance would be displayed.

A workaround I discovered:

  • Click on All Networks at the top
  • Click on Testnet and then again on Mainnets and return to wallet

That makes all zero-balance coins appear in view. They may disappear again, but at least you can copy an addresses to fund it and make it a non-zero balance address, or repeat the workaround to view them again at any time.

Why do all this?​

The display problem is temporary and maybe you won't even notice it.

Why bother in the first place? Several reasons.

Supra DEXes​

WXX in your StarKey will (eventually) be trade-able on Supra DEXes.

Currently, one is online (Dexlyn) and more are coming (Atmos, for example).

But again - why bother?

  • Low cost: Dexlyn's DEX charges 0.3% and transaction fees are negligible
  • Fast: transactions take seconds
  • The first native stablecoin on Supra is Dexlyn's dexUSDC, which is USDC (ERC-20) wrapped on Supra L1 using Dexlyn's Ethereum bridge, so you will be able to
    • Trade xx coin for dexUSDC
    • Exit/enter with WXX and USDC (ERC-20)
info

Dexlyn DEX does not yet support a variety of coins - it just launched last week.

The above will be possible when generic ERC-20 coins/tokens become supported on Dexlyn DEX. You would then add your own pairs (such as dexWXX/dexUSDC).

Alternatively - see below - wait for Supra's own cross-chain support.

As an example of low fees, here's an example of a transaction involving Dexlyn's Ethereum bridge.

On Supra L1 side, the fee was US$0.000. Affordable, right?

As you can see the exact amount is 0.001001 SUPRA ($0.00001742). Transaction fees will go up as L1 gets more busy, but even if they go up 10 times, it'll still be cheap! Did I mention that transactions complete in low single digit seconds?

What else should you know?

Your StarKey WXX can be sent to any wallet that supports it, and private key of the address can be exported if you want to simply import the address to another wallet.

For Dexlyn's DEX, they have instructions how to fund dexUSDC by sending USDC (ERC-20) to their bridge.

The only thing missing is dexWXX-dexUSDC pair and the correspoinding Liquidity Pool.

info

If you want to stay up-to-date, follow Dexlyn and Aptos on X.

  • Dexlyn: https://dexlyn.com/
  • Aptos: https://aptos.dev/

Next key step for Delxyn - in the context of this post - is to add support for arbitrary DIY pairs and pools. To trade WXX on Dexlyn DEX we'd need to wrap it, and trade dexWXX/dexUSDC.

For Aptos, it's to launch.

Later on when arbitrary pairs become available, we should be able to wrap WXX on Supra into something like dexWXX and trade/swap/send/receive natively on Supra L1.

info

In a week or so EVM will be available on Supra testnet.

Technically savvy folks could take the WXX contract (published by the XX Foundation) and release $SXX (Supra-wrapped XX on Supra L1) on Supra testnet.

xx coin and Supra ecosystem​

Supra is just getting started. It's 10 weeks old.

What's coming soon is:

  • Native EVM support (the flagship language is Move, and MoveVM is the first VM supported since TGE), which means the Ethereum Smart Contract used to issue WXX could be issued on Supra L1 without using Ethereum L1
  • Bridgeless (yes, bridgeless) cross-chain swapping on Supra. When a Substate chain (probably Polkadot or Kusama) becomes supported, xx Network could be supported as well, but xx Network would have to update chain to support latest and greatest Substrate SDK features

Either of these would be a huge improvement over WXX.

In the first case, with an SdexXX (assuming this be the name of a xx ERC-20 token created on Supra L1 using the same contract that was used for WXX) it would take seconds to exit to native xx tokens. And it'd cost almost nothing. Sound good?

In the second case, WXX would become redundant and xx Network wouldn't even need a bridge.

SupraNS​

Another interesting thing is SupraNS, the official Supra name system, i.e. "Supra's ENS". It's here and requires Supra tokens to register. It's much cheaper than ENS, which is great, but because it just launched there's no wallet that supports it.

info

StarKey will likely be the first to support SupraNS.

With SupraNS support in StarKey it will be easier to send tokens to Supra wallet.

Later on, when SupraNS allows for more entries (such as URLs and maybe arbitrary fields), we may be able to add things like nicknames from xx Network Haven (the reference quantum-resistant messaging app).

What changed, how and why​

Those who follow xx Network are likely aware of the changes to the minimum validator stake.

Until era 1140 (today), validators could start validating with only 1,000 XX (less than $50).

The intent to introduce changes and "return" to the old limit were first approved in a referendum, and then a separate democracy vote approved the same change to chain variables deployed as a scheduled task set to increase the minimum stake to 15,000 XX on January 1, 2025. The first referendum also approves additional hikes in 2025 as necessary.

The minimum used to be 14,0000 XX back in 2021 when XX token was worth 4-5 times as much.

Why was it reduced to 1,000 XX? Because several loud community members who don't know anything about economics made more noise than the rest.

With the new hike the minimum stake is slightly higher, but only in XX coins. In US dollars, it's still almost 80% lower than the already low stake that the network used to have before.

Why is 15K better than 1K​

Because xx Network is as Proof of Stake network.

Validators must have a lot at stake to demonstrate their commitment to good, secure validation. It's nothing new.

The new minimum stake will make it harder (more expensive) to scam nominators. Starting with tomorrow's era it will cost 15 times as much to stand up a bunch of nodes, wait until they're nominated and elected, and then hike commission from the initial "below 20%" standard to 30% or 40% until nominators figure it out. Once they do, you spin up a new node with just $50 on it and start again.

It will also make node hoarding more expensive. You can still run a 20 node validator farm and undermine the security of the network, but for new adversaries that becomes 15 times more expensive.

The hike doesn't "solve" either of these problems, but it makes such behavior more expensive.

What now​

Some of the same xx community ignoramuses claim 15,000 XX is already unbearable for the "poor validators" who haven't been making profits from validation and now won't be able to afford 14,000 XX to be able to continue validating.

First, that's false, because they can keep going as long as they don't stop validating.

Second, if validating is not sustainable, compromising security of the network to inflate participation (and create more money-losing validators) isn't a good way to fix that problem.

Tomorrow's Phragmen election will be the first one which will prevent new entrants, but all the existing ones will be able to continue with their sub-15K XX stake.

Of course, the scammers have their little VM-based nodes ready to go: among the 24 nodes currently in Waiting only three seem regulars. I checked each prospective validator's wallet: all but three had 1,010 XX or less staked!

What else needs to be done​

xx Network suffers from the price of the token which makes validation unprofitable. That cannot (or at least: should not) be fixed by compromising the security of the network.

But the community is disengaged (I wrote about the same problem seen in other DAOs such as Polkadot) and 90% of active community members are either "degens" (price-focused speculators) or economic ignoramuses, which makes any improvements difficult.

The xx Foundation and Technical Committee members are staying out of these (seemingly "non-fundamental") governance discussions which is appropriate, but the end result is not satisfactory because meaningful improvements cannot happen in such environment. And more needs to be done.

Validator slots should be shrunk, but the groups I mentioned are against because it's (supposedly) "bad PR". By that logic adding 10 more slots should help (after all, there's as many as 24 nodes in waiting!), but they don't suggest that. I wonder why that is.

Take-aways​

note

15,000 XX is a laughable minimum validator stake ($750).

It takes much more on other Substrate networks and $10,000 to validate on Cellframe.

Keep that in mind before you start "proving" that a node owner with $750 staked on the node has more at staken than a node owner with $10,000 stakend on the node.

As we are about to enter era 1141, the hike seems entirely justified and obviously helpful - it doesn't need to "solve" anything, it just needs to make the undermining of security more expensive, which it does by a factor of 15.

In other words, this remains possible but now costs 15 times more, which is great.

What else should be done?​

  • Validator slots should be cut by 10 or 20 slots to ensure nodes require at least 300 K XX to get elected. Now a handful of nodes has less than 100 K XX (probably due to the pool scammers reshuffling those nodes which dropped), although more recently it was around 225 K XX. The network didn't need 370 nodes in the first place and they shouldn't have been added, so there should be no second thoughts about making necessary reductions and yet here we are, with dozens of people running many nodes with 1K XX validator stake on each...
  • An xx Postage bounty should be created and released as soon as ongoing bounties are delivered, to ensure real demand for tokens in second half of 2025. A RFC is on the forum and just needs to be worked on and released.
  • Various governance improvements are necessary

Governance needs to be improved to create more engagement both internally (validators, nominators), but also externally (with builders). That is mostly happening because of this stalemate between a disengaged majority, a loud (but grossly incompetent) minority of validators, and an unopinionated Foundation. I hope this will be resolved and improved in 2025.

Update for era 1141​

  • Just two (non-scammers) from the Waiting list got elected. As I've said above, I'm not sure if the scammers were even considered, given their non-elected status and a sub-15 K XX validator stake.
  • The next question is how many validators are still running with a sub-15K XX stake and do not have other problems? The answer to this question is: not many. These are nodes that don't have the validator stake of 15,000 XX or higher:
    • No on-chain ID: these are often pools or soft-scammers (see the pattern above) who don't care about validator reputation
    • Too few nominators (may be high risk, although in some cases you'll find an old-timer who is self-sufficient nd doesn't have 3rd party nominators).     • High commission (scammers and self-stakers) - not worth nominating (scammers) or don't need nominators because they're sufficient (self-stakers, sometimes also pools)
    • High nominator stake (example: 6ZNPt8…i17kW8 at the very top, over 1 MXX) - not worth nominating, don't need nominators and obviously loaded in any case

Appendix B - potentially problematic nodes​

These shouldn't be a priori excluded, but are suspicious.

  • high commission
  • pool trash (CRYPTOCALIBUR, MONEYTEAM, UNITED EARTH, etc.) and non-mixing nodes (XX FOUNDATION are Foundation nodes that always get elected, but don't run cMixx)
  • too few or too many nominators (but see Note below)
  • low own stake (could stop validating, become unelectable, and leave your nomination inactive)
  • high nomination value (which means a lower ROI)
  • no on-chain ID (potential scammers, pool drones)
note

"Too few" nominators isn't a sure sign of a problem because that figure is the number of "staking nominators in current era" whereas the node may have more nominators but not assigned by Phragmen in this era. That would require additional checks. A self-sufficient runner (e.g. ANODE2 self-staked with 500 K XX) may have no nominators for obvious and perfectly legitimate reasons.

Introduction​

The idea is to crowdsource funds for a CrowdSwap liquidity pool for WXX (BRC20), which is a wrapped WXX (ERC20), which itself is wrapped XX on Ethereum.

Since BNB and Ethereum are similar, re-wrapping WXX (ERC20) - issued by the xx Foundation - is secure and easy.

Now some of the WXX (ERC20) that's available on Uniswap will be re-wrapped as WXX (BRC20), as I understand it, making it easy to move liquidity between DEXes. Pancake swap will be added later.

Read about it here.

warning
  • If you're a US citizen, US resident or from a weird country, don't even try (there's KYC)
  • Payment can be done today or on February 23, but "delivery" will happen after (February 25?) (see the link above)

This is not a recommendation or advice. Do your own research.

The sale ends on February 24, 2025.

Why buy WXX(BRC20)​

My interest here is simple:

  • I'm a believer in DEX
  • xx token hasn't been able to get listed at large centralized exchanges because its privacy options make it look fishy (as if it's a privacy coin, which it is not)
  • Historically DEXes have had a problem with liquidity. Newest one don't, but these still do, so they need help
  • There are rewards (funded by xx Foundation), so it's not just free donation of liquidity - there are benefits for those who participate to help with liquidity of decentralized XX markets

Buying process​

Read the link above for the details, but ignoring the conditions (very important, so you need to read the link), the "doing" part goes like this:

  • You need at least the equivalent of $105 or so in crypto, as the minimum purchase is $100 and there are other fees involved
  • Pick a wallet which can work with https://app.crowdswap.org and connect it
  • In user profile, fill in a name, email, and pick username for Crowdswap (I think that's mostly for referrals, there's no login or password)
  • Find the xx private sale for WXX on Opportunity page
  • You need to agree and "sign" an agreement to be able to actually perform a transaction
  • If you're not in one of trouble countries, a simple KYC may be necessary (for me it was very a painless process, but it may depend on your IP or who knows what)

Transactions​

Metamask was a frustrating experience for UDST (I could have used Ethereum, but I didn't want to pay their gas fees), so I gave up and used BNB chain in another wallet.

tip

The easiest way is to send ETH or BNB to a native ETH or BNB address wallet, and buy with that.

If you're looking for cheapest fees, maybe try sending USDT(BNB) or BNB tokens to a BNB wallet.

I don't know what networks are supported, so I can't really tell for sure. Please check on your own and test with a small transaction before you send $100 or more.

I screwed up at first - I sent USDT to a BNB wallet, but didn't have any native coins to do anything that, so I also had to send some dust for the gas. Stupid...

Tips for degens​

To fund my purchases of WXX(BRC20) I may sell some of my "non-wrapped" XX coins.

As others will likely do the same, it's is expected that the price of XX/USDT on MEXC (the biggest XX CEX market) will remain the same as the crowdsale price.

So... Do not buy WXX (or XX) thinking it's some crazy pump & dump scheme. It's not.

Why is this cool?​

  • Liquidity for decentralized XX token markets
  • Users new to xx Network will become familiar with DEX and cMixx (very important, as opposed to airdrops)
  • Affordable campaign to reach out to new users
  • Technical simplicity (uses existing XX-to-WXX contract on Ethereum)

Downsides:

  • As long as the sale lasts, it puts a ceiling on the price of xx token, because the sale is based on the fixed priced of xx token (I guess it had to be done that way), so MEXC and other markets will be "stuck" in a narrow range and miss the ongoing bubble. I don't care, but some folks do.

I think overall it's a very positive development for xx token.

Next steps​

These DEXes (Uniswap, Crowdswap, and in the future Pacakeswap) lessen the need for T1/T2 CEX.

note

Next-generation DEXes will do even more to balance power beteween crypto projects and CEXes. Keep an eye on that!

Why Polkadot governance sucks

December 15, 2024 · 7 min read
ArmchairAncap

  • Symptoms
  • Why Polkadot governance sucks?
  • What should Polkadot do?

Introduction​

I'll make this one short because there aren't many such opinions.

Polkadot is a DAO, and has governance built-in.

It is actually very well done and saves a lot of time to anyone building a blockchain-based DAO - xx Network uses it as well.

What, then, is the problem?

Symptoms​

My knowledge of Polkadot is somewhat limited - I haven't participated and used it for years.

But I've followed it closely this year and I've used Kusama, for example.

xx Network is also a DAO and uses essentially the same stack, so there isn't much an xx Network user needs to learn infrastructure-wise. What is needed to understand is observation - seeing how it's used, what users think about it, and so on.

Based on those observations, I've noticed several symptoms:

  • Claims that "whale" projects are milking the system by (easily) getting (undeserved) funding
  • Opinions that many DAO-funded projects suck
  • Dissatisfaction with how the DAO "democracy" works in practice
  • Observations on disengaged participation (example: people nominate, but don't vote on anything even as they login to wallet to collect rewards or transfer funds)
  • All sorts of stupid ideas are getting traction (and possibly DAO-approved funding - this I estimate based on seeing how much traction uterrly senseless ideas get in the Polkadot community)

Sadly, it didn't pass - probably because most of those with fat DOT wallets wouldn't want undue attention and similar reactions to their own proposals... ::

Here's another gem:

Literally every 3 days I have to vote on 20-30 new proposals. This is just so time consuming, so I just open the ICP voting webtab on a side screen, vote “YES” on the about 25 new proposals about every 3 days, not even read the stuff anylonger, while continuing doing other work on my main screen.

In other words, these are the same weaknesses that we can observe in Western democracies.

Shockingly, or perhaps not at all, some still claim the DAO is functioning "exceptionally well". I guess I'd claim that as well if I was milking the system to my satisfaction.

The obvious truth is governance is not functioning well at all.

Why Polkadot governance sucks?​

Here's why:

  • the DAO destroys, rather than creates, competition [1]
  • the DAO picks winners
  • the DAO thereby creates losers (once a winner for is picked, all others are fxxed because unless they're vastly superior they stand no chance of charging for their service and their funding dries up)
  • the DAO causes inflation with such unnecessary and excessive spending
  • the DAO hurts the ecosystem because it retards free markets that let users vote with their (subscription) tokens. Services that would normally compete and pass their costs (plus some extra value) to users siply don't appear because of this more-or-less "winner takes all" system created by the DAO
  • Everyone gets to vote, and that includes self-serving cronies, ignoramuses and people who have no idea about anything (many may be underage, although their stakes are usually (and thanfully) too low to matter)

[1] There's competition in the Art of Proposals and vote marshaling, all right, just not in projects and services

My forked (upstream was funded by the DAO, I suppose?) project for token-gated access to Web3 apps is a good example. I think the idea is great and use cases many (as I explain in that blog post). If you look around, you'll see there have been many asks for this functionality. For example, Discord channels could be gated the same way based on one's holdings of tokens, assets or NFTs. Even Web2 forums could use it.

Yet, no one maintains such an app for Polkadot, and "small" Web3 sites don't even exist. It's a simple app and people funded by the DAO like to apply for $500 grants rather than smaller projects that general users could use to create a "no-code" Web3 experience or bridge Web2 apps to Web3.

Another example is various "major" Web sites in the Polkadot ecosystem. They're barely useful and often bloated (more "features" means more funds).

Anyway, how to fix it? The same way it's done in Western democracies: by educating the voter, which is a centuries-long process and not something that will happen in our lifetime.

For a comparison, I think xx Network works better. It's not good enough, but it's not a disaster either.

Why is that? I think it's because it has better people, which is how it was bootstrapped. This isn't to say Polkadot has bad people, but it's been a while and they don't seem as engaged in the DAO as the original xx Network team.

xx Network is more of a constitutional republic, while Polkadot is a democracy. Even though both use the same code, the latter is already corrupt beyond repair. The USA vs. the European Union.

What should Polkadot do?​

To be honest, I don't know how they can get enough votes to do anything but keep destroying what the founder(s) and early volunteers built.

note

On that note, those who "volunteered" didn't do it for free. Most had interest in making DOT and KSM appreciate in value. Today's DAO leeches are nothing like the early volunteers.

If you're contributing only as long as they pay you, you're more akin to a bureaucrat or public sector worker. Not everyone is, but most are.

Some minor improvements can be made - for example they recently modified inflation rate, which is now less stupid. But I don't think majority of voters would support any proposal that significantly impacts their disproportional or undeserved benefits. It's like expecting public sector unions to support budget cuts.

Usually a short-term solution - or interim solution - is to have a benevolent populist dictator. Someone like Linus Torvalds, who is liked enough to be given extra power. That's great as long as the person is around, and goes south quickly as soon as he gets replaced by a lesser person.

In addition to that being a short-term solution, at this point it the DAO can't even do that because there's no mechanism for this to happen. It's wrongly bootstrapped/wired.

Whether it's done by a dictator or a powerful group, here's what I think would fix a lot of problems:

  • Burn all excess treasury funds not required for basic expenses plus some emergency reserve
  • Eliminate funding of most projects excluding essential infrastructure (such as a bare bones blockchain explorer) and let them compete for paying customers on their own
  • Reorganize funding so that it's split by categories and done only over very short term (3 to 6 months - just enough to let them do a PoC and be able to seek funding). Then modify voting so that those who support funding be mildly on the hook (use long-term conviction proportional to cost of spending they choose to support)
  • Strengthen the role of on-chain identity to bring more transparency to decision-making and spending
  • Extend unstaking period to months and years, so that it becomes lucrative to vote with higher conviction and divest slower

Each of these items is impossible to happen, but even if it did, it wouldn't last long: cronies and ingnoramuses would start taking over again, possibly starting at the top (with someone who'd undo everything even faster).

Take-away​

The same tool used by different people can lead to a very different result.

The Polkadot DAO is failing, but that doesn't mean it's worse than other crypto projects. Most other projects are much worse, which is why I'm happy to be part of the greater Polkadot ecosystem and xx Network in particular.

Real and sustainable change requires education, which takes decades and maybe even centuries. We won't live long enough to see it.

Meanwhile, those of us who care have to do what we can to slow that decline and make our projects stay around long enough until most "DAOists" learn to act in common interest.
