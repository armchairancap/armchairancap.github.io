Intro​

Some people who advocate DEXes are decentralization fanatics, others are conspiracy theorists, and some "all of the above".

There are also pragmatists in this group. In this post I share my pragmatic arguments in favor of moving your active stash to a DEX.

Note that this is specific to Supra and my own purpose, which is to see the project grow and, with it, the value it provides to its ecosystem. More value means more growth and a higher coin price. I view Supra as my "enterprise" blockchain holding: I'd like it to see grow as a normal for-profit business would.

note

I elaborate because I also hold xx coin which is more of a "cause coin" for me and where I'm not pragmatic in terms of the token price, earnings and such.

In the case of xx coin, I'm interested in "economically-sustainable private messaging" and have very different concerns.

In the case of Supra, I don't think centralization is bad. I simply don't like how these particular unregulated guys run their business.

Another special circumstance is Supra is a good L1 platform for DEX markets, so I also have - all Supra holders and stakers should have - a conflict of interest in their mind.

Current Supra-listing CEXes​

I don't expect to add anything new here, as cryptocurrency DEXes have been around for a decade or more, and pragmatic arguments against CEXes have been around longer.

Briefly, from the viewpoint explained above (meaning: pragmatic, Supra-specific arguments from an enterprise POV)...

  • Lack of transparency: this is really the worst part
  • Poor business practices: we don't know who and how (why, because no transparency) not only in terms of transparency, but also in terms of governance
  • Leverage (Futures): this isn't bad in itself, but I think it's a major tool used in market manipulation

Regarding the lack of transparency, folks on X like to make fun of crypto/DeFi fans saying the folks who claimed zero regulation was good, start whining when their altcoin gets smashed on the same markets. I agree with them:

  • I may decide to participate in a zero-regulation market (a DEX, for example) and I won't whine if lose
  • Zero-regulation (or "extremely lax" regulation) CEX could work if these exchanges voluntarily shared data that should be shared. But they don't share anything of substance, while regulated CEXes probably collect and share too much, which increases costs for no benefit to users. There's nothing "in-between" which is a shame
info

CEXes also have some good sides and advantages. For example, most of these are still true:

  • Low latency, very suitable for frequent trading (exchange)
  • Better features for advanced traders (such as leverage, automation, APIs, clients)
  • Convenience

CEXes still command close to 80% of crypto spot trading and there's nothing wrong with using them. People say "not your private keys not your coins", but I would guess more people lost their coins in self-custody than at CEXes.

Governance​

Transparency is sometimes legally mandated, but for unregulated exchanges it's merely a choice.

There's nothing that prevents them from busting manipulators, reporting money launderes, and such. From what I can tell the unregulated CEXes where Supra is listed are doing the bare minimum.

Another area of governance is operations. If you work for a regulated FSI player you're simply not allowed to do certain things the weird or wrong way. There are checks and checks of checks.

For example, using plain text passwords could be grounds for dismissal, and you'll get discovered because Ops don't run Security. But the ByBit guy transferred $1.5B to the North Korean hackers because ByBit used a DIY workflow "invented" by ByBit. Had they followed practices in place in the UK or US, it'd be slower, safer and more expensive. They probably didn't like the speed and cost and thought they were smarter than hackers...

This argument applies to DEXes as well (were bugs may go undetected due to corner cutting in development, operations or auditing), but it's not an argument against regulated CEXes. Unfortunately no Western CEX list Supra at this time, so there's no "better" CEX to go to.

Perpetual Futures aka Perps​

Perpetual futures are basically entries in a database which is a closed zero-sum system where there is no Supra involved at all.

In fact, there's no USD involved either! Yes, it says SUPRA/USDT, but understand that the market doesn't need either.

  • A DB is created, and named after a pair such as SUPRA/USDT
  • To play, gamers deposit USDT "at the entrance" in order to be able to enter and play, but just like Supra (the other side of this pair), the Perps DB does not "use" USDT. It's just a database
  • Next, players play against each other, and the survivors "unlock" thei USDT deposit and collect deposit of other users who've lost, while the CEX retains trading revenues
  • Players could deposit collateral in gemstones, Litecoin or something else. USDT is simply what's most convenient in this case because it maps 1:1 to a side in this pair
  • Players cannot take Supra out of this market, because there's no Supra, as we've said earlier

Next, realize that Perps provide leverage

There are two interesting things about this.

The first is, players can deposit 10 USDT "at the entrance" and play with 500 USDT. This speeds up the CEX'es "time to fleecing". Notice how it also makes $500 available to CEX interest-free. It's a closed system so as long as they liquidate overleveraged positions in time, there's no chance for the CEX to lose here. This is obviously much better (for CEXes) and worse for customers. Instead of hesitating to send users to Futures section, they employ "dark UI patterns" to attract users to Perps. MEXC won't even show Spot in top menu once you enter Futures!

Point 2: with a 50x leverage you can buy, but also sell, 50x more Supra than you could normally sell. That means Perps are the place to go to smash a project or simply cause price-impacting swings in Spot SUPRA/USDT as "the tail is wagging the dog".

But it's even worse than that! We said "50x more Supra than you could normally sell", but in order to sell you'd have to buy. With Perps not only do you can sell 50x more, but you don't have to buy any at all. You deposit USDT at the entrance, lever up 40x, smack Supra, and exit with more USDT in your pockets.

You may wonder why this doesn't equally work for buyers. The truth is it does, but that doesn't make this setup better. If marketplace owner determines you're trading in a way meant to manipulate Spot prices - for example you're shorting in Perps with 40x and and have buy orders in Spot - they should step in and do something about that. These cowboy CEXes do not.

DEX benefits to you and Supra ecosystem​

I'll keep this simple because it ought to be obvious and it was explained at the top:

  • Better transparency, fairness and more disclosure than unregulated CEXes
  • Convenience, performance and features are very close - it's good enough for non-professionals
  • Supra-based DEXes trade on-chain, chain earns revenue, more people buy Supra to pay for those fees
  • Supra-based DEXes need liquidity. You can stake DEX pools and make money doing that

It's also a way to distribute risk for your "active" Supra stash - part of your tokens that moves more often, as opposed to staked Supra that is likely quite static. You may do something like this:

  • 1/3 of active stash on a regulated CEX if you feel the need to day trade from time to time (some people prefer unregulatd; I do too, but not any of the current ones)
  • 1/3 in Staking or LP Staking on Supra-based DEX number 1 (e.g. Dexlyn)
  • 1/3 on Staking or LP Staking on Supra-based DEX number 2 (e.g. Atmos)

Slippage and fees​

Everyone knows Supra is fast and transaction costs are very low, Supra's DORA (Distributed Oracles) provides up to date price feeds quickly, but one "sensitive" bit about DEXes is slippage and fees.

There isn't enough independent detailed information available to compare, but anecodtally:

  • An unregulated CEX may have a 0.5% "taker" fee
  • Dexlyn may have a 0.3% commission, and there's "slippage" that's to be kept at below 0.5% (default)

The problem is we don't really know how it behaves (slippage). From my experience with smaller (below $1,000) amounts, Dexlyn worked fine when I compared the price from that UI with CEX prices in another browser.

info

Maybe this doesn't work that great with larger orders, but I haven't seen any evidence, so I can't conclude much from unsubstantiated "concern trolling".

Yes, maybe, if you Swap $10,000today you'd be hit with 2% slippage. I don't know, but it's possible.. But don't do $10K at a time if you notice at $2K it's already higher than 0.5%!

For normal retail use swapping on Dexlyn right now will cost you up to 0.8% (up to 0.5% slippage plus fixed 0.3% commission). It can be below 0.5% - less than you pay at a low-cost deregulated CEX!

This may change for better or worse, but it's quite simple and low-risk: don't use a crap DEX you've never heard of and run small tests before ramping up like a degen! Transactions take seconds, so I'm sure you can spend 30 seconds trying with $10, $50, and $100 before you try with $500. They also have a Testnet to try with fake coins first.

Supra Network makes it possible to address most of those problems, so while slippage has historically been a week spot for DEXes, it's not supposed to happen on Supra if DEXes make good use of those L1 features.

SupraNS (SNS) names with hexadecimal symbols

March 19, 2025 · 3 min read
ArmchairAncap

Today I registered some hex names.

They're not just "fancy" but have use cases, one of which I just added to SupraNS. Such names are not novel, but they're rarely used and, in my opinion, interesting. So I'll write a few words about that.

Say you get a SNs name that consists of hexadecimal symbols ([0-9][A-F]). Example: b00b5.

Is that interesting and/or useful? Let's take a closer look.

Downsides of hex SNS names​

A "hex name" can be dangerous if you get autocorrected when entering such SNS names. For example, a payee may end up entering boobs.supra in StarKey wallet and send funds to the wrong address.

But that risk depends on the name and whether the "wrong" SNS exists (in many cases it would). Most names shouldn't get autocorrected (especially not b00b5). In some cases hex names will be less autocorrect-prone because they may not match common misspellings (thanks to the fact that they usually contain digits).

Another danger is visual confusion, where someone misreads 1abe1.supra and sends funds to label.supra. One workaround is to register the both SNS names. Another is to not make such mistakes (e.g. use a good font, or advise your counterparty to use copy-and-paste rather than view-and-retype when entering your SNS hex address).

Benefits of hex SNS names​

First, they're fancy and can be c001 (somebody snapped this one before me).

Second, they cay be practical. If you create a wallet address that matches your SupraNS name for easy address recognition (e.g. in SupraScan.io or API output), it's easier to use or develop. For example, I may have SNS b00b5.supra mapped to wallet address b00b57fef64e98e838eef231215dd0e7fa634d2fd70e75ae50df3a2959eba2f4.

Or, the proverbial Alice may be boob5 + a, which allows me up to 26 test addresses for easy testing:

alice.b00b5.supra # b00b5a7fef64e98e838eef231215dd0e7fa634d2fd70e75ae50df3a2959eba2f
jane.b00b5.supra  # b00b5j16c6bde199856efd1204ae840d21e3a26d0c501fb1521a0d9f7302934d
note

Note that StarKey won't create vanity addresses for you. Even if it could, it'd be slow for anything but 3 letters.

You'd have to generate such addresses outside and import their private key to StarKey. You don't need that for SupraScan.io, CLI or API use cases.

Other notes​

SNS names can be as short as 3 single-byte characters.

Long hex names are impractical: hard to read, hard to tyype. If you combine two "hex words" (say, b19b00b5), that's probably the upper limit. Three words are usually hard to read. And - isn't a hexadecimal symbol, so b19-b00b5 is possible in SNS but can't be mapped to a wallet address.

The last bit is related to those who want to use vanity addresses. Today it's still quite hard to generate them for 7 or more characters.

Therefore I recommend SNS length of hex names to be in the range 3-6 (characters). If you don't plan to use vanity addresses, then up to 10(-ish) may be fine, assuming you don't pick a lame name.

Where to buy or register hex names​

I registered a bunch of hex names according to the above considerations: most are 3-6 characters and therefore suitable for pairing with vanity addresses. Some are longer, but I registered only those long hex names that I really liked.

You can find them by sorting the table of available names by Comment and looking for comments that contain "hex".

Some good ones (including 3 letter names) are still available through Dexlyn's SupraNS dApp.

Update (March 25, 2025)​

SupraNS names are now transferable directly from Dexlyn's dApp UI.

There's no marketplace yet, but we have been gifting and exchanging names in a trusted way.

Add WXX to StarKey​

warning

Make a backup of your StarKey pass phrase. Like with other non-custodial walles, there's no other way to get access.

The pencil icon is where you toggle display (ON/OFF) of existing tokens. But WXX does not exist, so we need to add it.

Once in Manage Token page, you can toggle other tokens (Solana, etc.), but to add WXX you need to click on the + icon to add a custom token.

This is where the WXX contract is needed:

  • Network: Ethereum
  • Contract: paste the WXX contract address you got earlier

The rest will be auto-populated.

You have added WXX!

Now you can go back to Manage Tokens (the pencil icon). Use the magnifying glass icon to find WXX and click the star to make the token a favorite.

Finally, we can click on WXX to receive or send (ETH is required for transaction fees) WXX.

tip

I encountered a problem with StarKey v2.1.2 in this last step: no coins - built-in or manually added - with zero balance would be displayed.

A workaround I discovered:

  • Click on All Networks at the top
  • Click on Testnet and then again on Mainnets and return to wallet

That makes all zero-balance coins appear in view. They may disappear again, but at least you can copy an addresses to fund it and make it a non-zero balance address, or repeat the workaround to view them again at any time.

The display problem is temporary and maybe you won't even notice it.

    • Exit/enter with WXX and USDC (ERC-20)

What changed, how and why​

Those who follow xx Network are likely aware of the changes to the minimum validator stake.

Until era 1140 (today), validators could start validating with only 1,000 XX (less than $50).

The intent to introduce changes and "return" to the old limit were first approved in a referendum, and then a separate democracy vote approved the same change to chain variables deployed as a scheduled task set to increase the minimum stake to 15,000 XX on January 1, 2025. The first referendum also approves additional hikes in 2025 as necessary.

The minimum used to be 14,0000 XX back in 2021 when XX token was worth 4-5 times as much.

Why was it reduced to 1,000 XX? Because several loud community members who don't know anything about economics made more noise than the rest.

With the new hike the minimum stake is slightly higher, but only in XX coins. In US dollars, it's still almost 80% lower than the already low stake that the network used to have before.

Why is 15K better than 1K​

Because xx Network is as Proof of Stake network.

Validators must have a lot at stake to demonstrate their commitment to good, secure validation. It's nothing new.

The new minimum stake will make it harder (more expensive) to scam nominators. Starting with tomorrow's era it will cost 15 times as much to stand up a bunch of nodes, wait until they're nominated and elected, and then hike commission from the initial "below 20%" standard to 30% or 40% until nominators figure it out. Once they do, you spin up a new node with just $50 on it and start again.

It will also make node hoarding more expensive. You can still run a 20 node validator farm and undermine the security of the network, but for new adversaries that becomes 15 times more expensive.

The hike doesn't "solve" either of these problems, but it makes such behavior more expensive.

What now​

Some of the same xx community ignoramuses claim 15,000 XX is already unbearable for the "poor validators" who haven't been making profits from validation and now won't be able to afford 14,000 XX to be able to continue validating.

First, that's false, because they can keep going as long as they don't stop validating.

Second, if validating is not sustainable, compromising security of the network to inflate participation (and create more money-losing validators) isn't a good way to fix that problem.

Tomorrow's Phragmen election will be the first one which will prevent new entrants, but all the existing ones will be able to continue with their sub-15K XX stake.

Of course, the scammers have their little VM-based nodes ready to go: among the 24 nodes currently in Waiting only three seem regulars. I checked each prospective validator's wallet: all but three had 1,010 XX or less staked!

What else needs to be done​

xx Network suffers from the price of the token which makes validation unprofitable. That cannot (or at least: should not) be fixed by compromising the security of the network.

But the community is disengaged (I wrote about the same problem seen in other DAOs such as Polkadot) and 90% of active community members are either "degens" (price-focused speculators) or economic ignoramuses, which makes any improvements difficult.

The xx Foundation and Technical Committee members are staying out of these (seemingly "non-fundamental") governance discussions which is appropriate, but the end result is not satisfactory because meaningful improvements cannot happen in such environment. And more needs to be done.

Validator slots should be shrunk, but the groups I mentioned are against because it's (supposedly) "bad PR". By that logic adding 10 more slots should help (after all, there's as many as 24 nodes in waiting!), but they don't suggest that. I wonder why that is.

Take-aways​

note

15,000 XX is a laughable minimum validator stake ($750).

It takes much more on other Substrate networks and $10,000 to validate on Cellframe.

Keep that in mind before you start "proving" that a node owner with $750 staked on the node has more at staken than a node owner with $10,000 stakend on the node.

As we are about to enter era 1141, the hike seems entirely justified and obviously helpful - it doesn't need to "solve" anything, it just needs to make the undermining of security more expensive, which it does by a factor of 15.

In other words, this remains possible but now costs 15 times more, which is great.

What else should be done?​

  • Validator slots should be cut by 10 or 20 slots to ensure nodes require at least 300 K XX to get elected. Now a handful of nodes has less than 100 K XX (probably due to the pool scammers reshuffling those nodes which dropped), although more recently it was around 225 K XX. The network didn't need 370 nodes in the first place and they shouldn't have been added, so there should be no second thoughts about making necessary reductions and yet here we are, with dozens of people running many nodes with 1K XX validator stake on each...
  • An xx Postage bounty should be created and released as soon as ongoing bounties are delivered, to ensure real demand for tokens in second half of 2025. A RFC is on the forum and just needs to be worked on and released.
  • Various governance improvements are necessary

Governance needs to be improved to create more engagement both internally (validators, nominators), but also externally (with builders). That is mostly happening because of this stalemate between a disengaged majority, a loud (but grossly incompetent) minority of validators, and an unopinionated Foundation. I hope this will be resolved and improved in 2025.

Update for era 1141​

  • Just two (non-scammers) from the Waiting list got elected. As I've said above, I'm not sure if the scammers were even considered, given their non-elected status and a sub-15 K XX validator stake.
  • The next question is how many validators are still running with a sub-15K XX stake and do not have other problems? The answer to this question is: not many. These are nodes that don't have the validator stake of 15,000 XX or higher:
    • No on-chain ID: these are often pools or soft-scammers (see the pattern above) who don't care about validator reputation
    • Too few nominators (may be high risk, although in some cases you'll find an old-timer who is self-sufficient nd doesn't have 3rd party nominators). These can easily move tokens from the nomination to the validation wallet and get compliant instantly (i.e. buy 15K XX on MEXC and withdraw to validator wallet and in parallel unbond 15K from nominator wallet and later sell that on MEXC)
    • High commission (scammers and self-stakers) - not worth nominating (scammers) or don't need nominators because they're sufficient (self-stakers, sometimes also pools)
    • High nominator stake (example: 6ZNPt8…i17kW8 at the very top, over 1 MXX) - not worth nominating, don't need nominators and obviously loaded in any case

Appendix B - potentially problematic nodes​

These shouldn't be a priori excluded, but are suspicious.

  • high commission
  • pool trash (CRYPTOCALIBUR, MONEYTEAM, UNITED EARTH, etc.) and non-mixing nodes (XX FOUNDATION are Foundation nodes that always get elected, but don't run cMixx)
  • too few or too many nominators (but see Note below)
  • low own stake (could stop validating, become unelectable, and leave your nomination inactive)
  • high nomination value (which means a lower ROI)
  • no on-chain ID (potential scammers, pool drones)
note

"Too few" nominators isn't a sure sign of a problem because that figure is the number of "staking nominators in current era" whereas the node may have more nominators but not assigned by Phragmen in this era. That would require additional checks. A self-sufficient runner (e.g. ANODE2 self-staked with 500 K XX) may have no nominators for obvious and perfectly legitimate reasons.
