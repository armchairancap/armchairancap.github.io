High-level Overview​

Blocking​

I've been developing and testing aproaches for blocking, reporting, etc.

The script - as posted on Github - works fine with generic ufw settings recommended in the xx Network Handbook, so you may use it by yourself if you want.

I'm pleased to report that zero (0) people joined my attempt to execute Plan A. (Just as expected, I might add - it's not that different from Polkadot governance issues described above.)

This is about my next attempt and this time I'll do it myself because both those who are paid to do something about it and xx Network governance have failed me.

Plan B vs Plan A​

With Plan A, I tried to fund others to block IPs of pool nodes. That was actually a great plan, but the community sucks and/or is disengaged.

With Plan B, I'm just going to do it myself. The idea is the same: annoy and economically hurt centralizing sock-puppet node farmers. To do that, I need to build a farm of blocking validator nodes.

note

But I also don't need to make a big impact. It's enough to get noticed, so that is key: get noticed and raise awareness about the problem.

If I make some pool runners give up or change node names to "hide" them, that would be even better.

Blocking and annoying centralizing pools can't fix the problem: the moment I stop doing this, they'll start coming back. It has to be fixed through the currently failing governance and the Foundation.

I don't run either of those, so all I can do is make noise about outside the community, because inside I've already tried and failed.

I may run this experiment for weeks or months, depending on how I feel about it.

  • I will stand up one or more validator node(s)
  • Validator commission will be low (I don't know how low, but likely single-digit)

Why Polkadot governance sucks

December 15, 2024 · 7 min read
ArmchairAncap
  • Introduction
  • Symptoms
  • Why Polkadot governance sucks?
  • What should Polkadot do?
  • Take-away

Introduction​

I'll make this one short because there aren't many such opinions.

Polkadot is a DAO, and has governance built-in.

It is actually very well done and saves a lot of time to anyone building a blockchain-based DAO - xx Network uses it as well.

What, then, is the problem?

Symptoms​

My knowledge of Polkadot is somewhat limited - I haven't participated and used it for years.

But I've followed it closely this year and I've used Kusama, for example.

xx Network is also a DAO and uses essentially the same stack, so there isn't much an xx Network user needs to learn infrastructure-wise. What is needed to understand is observation - seeing how it's used, what users think about it, and so on.

Based on those observations, I've noticed several symptoms:

  • Claims that "whale" projects are milking the system by (easily) getting (undeserved) funding
  • Opinions that many DAO-funded projects suck
  • Dissatisfaction with how the DAO "democracy" works in practice
  • Observations on disengaged participation (example: people nominate, but don't vote on anything even as they login to wallet to collect rewards or transfer funds)
  • All sorts of stupid ideas are getting traction (and possibly DAO-approved funding - this I estimate based on seeing how much traction uterrly senseless ideas get in the Polkadot community)
info

This proposal proposed a bounty to fund a lawsuit against a "notable" community member Giotto De Filippi who scammed the DAO with a fake memecoin proposal that was funded with 1 MM DOT.

Clowns...

Sadly, it didn't pass - probably because most of those with fat DOT wallets wouldn't want undue attention and similar reactions to their own proposals... ::

Here's another gem:

Literally every 3 days I have to vote on 20-30 new proposals. This is just so time consuming, so I just open the ICP voting webtab on a side screen, vote “YES” on the about 25 new proposals about every 3 days, not even read the stuff anylonger, while continuing doing other work on my main screen.

In other words, these are the same weaknesses that we can observe in Western democracies.

Shockingly, or perhaps not at all, some still claim the DAO is functioning "exceptionally well". I guess I'd claim that as well if I was milking the system to my satisfaction.

The obvious truth is governance is not functioning well at all.

Why Polkadot governance sucks?​

Here's why:

  • the DAO destroys, rather than creates, competition [1]
  • the DAO picks winners
  • the DAO thereby creates losers (once a winner for is picked, all others are fxxed because unless they're vastly superior they stand no chance of charging for their service and their funding dries up)
  • the DAO causes inflation with such unnecessary and excessive spending
  • the DAO hurts the ecosystem because it retards free markets that let users vote with their (subscription) tokens. Services that would normally compete and pass their costs (plus some extra value) to users siply don't appear because of this more-or-less "winner takes all" system created by the DAO
  • Everyone gets to vote, and that includes self-serving cronies, ignoramuses and people who have no idea about anything (many may be underage, although their stakes are usually (and thanfully) too low to matter)

[1] There's competition in the Art of Proposals and vote marshaling, all right, just not in projects and services

My forked (upstream was funded by the DAO, I suppose?) project for token-gated access to Web3 apps is a good example. I think the idea is great and use cases many (as I explain in that blog post). If you look around, you'll see there have been many asks for this functionality. For example, Discord channels could be gated the same way based on one's holdings of tokens, assets or NFTs. Even Web2 forums could use it.

Yet, no one maintains such an app for Polkadot, and "small" Web3 sites don't even exist. It's a simple app and people funded by the DAO like to apply for $500 grants rather than smaller projects that general users could use to create a "no-code" Web3 experience or bridge Web2 apps to Web3.

Another example is various "major" Web sites in the Polkadot ecosystem. They're barely useful and often bloated (more "features" means more funds).

Anyway, how to fix it? The same way it's done in Western democracies: by educating the voter, which is a centuries-long process and not something that will happen in our lifetime.

For a comparison, I think xx Network works better. It's not good enough, but it's not a disaster either.

Why is that? I think it's because it has better people, which is how it was bootstrapped. This isn't to say Polkadot has bad people, but it's been a while and they don't seem as engaged in the DAO as the original xx Network team.

xx Network is more of a constitutional republic, while Polkadot is a democracy. Even though both use the same code, the latter is already corrupt beyond repair. The USA vs. the European Union.

What should Polkadot do?​

To be honest, I don't know how they can get enough votes to do anything but keep destroying what the founder(s) and early volunteers built.

note

On that note, those who "volunteered" didn't do it for free. Most had interest in making DOT and KSM appreciate in value. Today's DAO leeches are nothing like the early volunteers.

If you're contributing only as long as they pay you, you're more akin to a bureaucrat or public sector worker. Not everyone is, but most are.

Some minor improvements can be made - for example they recently modified inflation rate, which is now less stupid. But I don't think majority of voters would support any proposal that significantly impacts their disproportional or undeserved benefits. It's like expecting public sector unions to support budget cuts.

Usually a short-term solution - or interim solution - is to have a benevolent populist dictator. Someone like Linus Torvalds, who is liked enough to be given extra power. That's great as long as the person is around, and goes south quickly as soon as he gets replaced by a lesser person.

In addition to that being a short-term solution, at this point it the DAO can't even do that because there's no mechanism for this to happen. It's wrongly bootstrapped/wired.

Whether it's done by a dictator or a powerful group, here's what I think would fix a lot of problems:

  • Burn all excess treasury funds not required for basic expenses plus some emergency reserve
  • Eliminate funding of most projects excluding essential infrastructure (such as a bare bones blockchain explorer) and let them compete for paying customers on their own
  • Reorganize funding so that it's split by categories and done only over very short term (3 to 6 months - just enough to let them do a PoC and be able to seek funding). Then modify voting so that those who support funding be mildly on the hook (use long-term conviction proportional to cost of spending they choose to support)
  • Strengthen the role of on-chain identity to bring more transparency to decision-making and spending
  • Extend unstaking period to months and years, so that it becomes lucrative to vote with higher conviction and divest slower

Each of these items is impossible to happen, but even if it did, it wouldn't last long: cronies and ingnoramuses would start taking over again, possibly starting at the top (with someone who'd undo everything even faster).

Take-away​

The same tool used by different people can lead to a very different result.

The Polkadot DAO is failing, but that doesn't mean it's worse than other crypto projects. Most other projects are much worse, which is why I'm happy to be part of the greater Polkadot ecosystem and xx Network in particular.

Real and sustainable change requires education, which takes decades and maybe even centuries. We won't live long enough to see it.

Meanwhile, those of us who care have to do what we can to slow that decline and make our projects stay around long enough until most "DAOists" learn to act in common interest.

Introduction​

How did we get here and why this hike was necessary​

Originally the minimum validator stake was much higher (14,000 XX) and that was when the coin was several times more valuable. Now the minimum stake is 1/14 of the amount and 1/4 of the value, which means in fiat terms it's 1/64-th of what it was.

Good Samaritans of Supra Network

December 8, 2024 · 8 min read
ArmchairAncap

"And who is my neighbor?"

J.H.C.

Situation​

Supra launched and the process of token distribution was a mess. (The network itself is promising.)

Among the problems, one of the small ones was that many "airdrop" users weren't able to "claim" their $SUPRA because they couldn't pay for the transaction fee required to do that.

Of course, that's not really a problem: open a CEX account, buy $SUPRA and send it to your address. 15 minutes of easy labor.

But why work 15 minutes, solve your problem and put yourself in a position in which you can also help your fellow users (1), when you can beg for free in Supra Discord instead?

(1) By this I mean residents of countries who cannot open accounts in currently available CEXes. None of the current tax haven CEXes accept US account holders, for example.

Good Samaritans​

From an economics perspective, there are several interesting things about this.

Discord admins​

Discord channel admins allowed begging, which is commonly banned everywhere because it doesn't add any value. It is also counterproductive because it adds noise, and doesn't help the project (whereas sending uses to CEXes does).

Community members​

First, I was suprised that relatively few people in Discord complained, but I think I know why: nobody bothers to read anything but their own threads and the lax moderation makes that worse becuase there's simply too many stupid posts in the channel (#general on the Supra Discord). At the same time, many are mad about the problems in the claim process, so the administrators can't be as strict as they should be.

Second, some users, like farmsenth.eth on X, offer to send 1 $SUPRA for "free" to anyone, to help them claim tokens.

The Supra guys reposted that, but I commented about this on X saying it's a terrible idea that doesn't help Supra at all. Later I noticed a change - they started telling users to go to a CEX and buy $SUPRA - so looks like my feedback had some effect.

I forgot to mention that feeding beggars just makes the problem worse because those who would reluctantly open a CEX account would decide to skip that and beg instead, seeing how it's allowed and works.

Third, there were two groups of people who didn't feed the beggars (as much):

  • Zero-interest lenders: those who offered to lend 1 $SUPRA under the condition that the borrower pays back after they claim
  • Predatory profiteers: those who offered to lend at a profit. I belong to this group.

Who's the bad guy here?​

People like farmsenth.eth are the worst, for reasons explained above and in the tweet. In this group, the Discord account souravrockz (maybe the same guy, I haven't looked) is particularly active, using free $SUPRAs as a marketing tool for gathering followers for his airdrop platform. If that's not definition of spamming, I don't know what is. But they let him anyway. farmsenth.eth does the same on X, buys followers while making CEX partners lose customers they probably justifiably expected to win when they invested in marketing and launch activities.

The lenders are not as bad, although they too do the same disservice to the network because they decrease liquidity by helping users stay "inactive" as far as CEXes are involved.

note

Note that users who claim tokens without opening a CEX account cannot actually do anything with them.

There's no staking on Supra as of now and there are no DEXes for $SUPRA either, so there's no reason to help them - they cannot possibly do anything with the coins if they do not create a CEX account.

The only difference between helping them and not helping them is if you help them they won't need to buy few more $SUPRA - they can dump the tokens without any fiat investment in the process.

Typical example: buying 1,000 and then immediately selling 790 $SUPRA leaves you with 210 $SUPRA that you can send to your wallet. Early on (within a week from the launch) that was less than a $2 in "additional investment". But the pikers were relentless. Why buy 210 when I can beg for 1?

How many supras in gas do I need to be able to claim bybit? It asks for a minimum purchase of 1000 supras. That is too much. Is there no way to get gas elsewhere? ~ beggar from Supra Discord

Profiteers such as I are less bad than the lenders because by asking to be compensated we discourage begging (since not all beggars want to pay more back).

I would prefer that beggars be banned or kicked so that there's no begging and everyone goes to CEX so that only a small minority (Americans and Europeans, mostly) asks for help. But begging is going on and the first two groups doing their thing, so I decided to participate using what I belive is the least bad approach.

Did it work?

  • Yes, when no one from the other two groups (donors and lenders) happens to be online
  • No, when someone from one or both groups offers to help out for free

Predatory lending?​

Is 500% interest per day predatory lending?

No, because it takes me minutes of work (copy, login, send, messaging) to do it and the "profit" was 8 $SUPRA (approximately 15 cents at the time) per customer.

That's not even US$ 9 per hour, so I worked for less than a minimum wage in the West while having capital at risk. Sorry, but I'm not sorry!

Results​

Another fun aspect of this has been profitability.

I've sent 2 $SUPRA to 4 people, each of whom agreed to pay me back 10 $SUPRA.

Only two paid back anything. From what I could tell, one was Chinese and one was American.

note

The user from China went above & beyond: he asked for 1 $SUPRA. I sent him 2 just to be sure he doesn't get stuck, and he sent me back 20 $SUPRA!

Maybe he assumed the original 1-for-10 deal meant I expected 20 in exchange for 2 and he didn't want to make a big deal out of it. Real gentleman!

I later sent him 9 $SUPRA to stick to the original deal (1 in exchange for 10).

These two wallet owners weren't as nice. They agreed to receive 2 and pay back 10 (approximately $0.015 and $0.15, respectively, at the time) and failed to deliver:

  • 0x699c017fe9b7112a98e58d9896eee3c8fc57ca985f161527c1aeeb491c2093b7
  • 0xf1610d90bbc83b967a9b993f05de1e0eca61d2bf45003d840cd3cd0a896638e0

They didn't tell me why they didn't pay back, informed me why they couldn't, or told me about some delay or anything like that.

From my side, I didn't ask them if they maybe "forgot" about the deal, but I'll gladly link their wallets here.

Another guy dropped out "in presales stage" - he wanted just 1 $SUPRA (not 2, which was stupid since the assumption was he'd return both anyway), but didn't commit to returning any, so I disqualified him.

The spammers and lenders did another 20-30 transactions that I saw (and probably 10x more when I wasn't looking), so one of my conclusions is certainly that people who really needed help were few.

The rest were simply lazy or outright scammers. Some probably didn't even have any $SUPRA to claim - they just wanted to profit from begging.

Since then $SUPRA went up a lot (it's $0.3 now), so I'd probably ask 2 $SUPRA in return for 5 $SUPRA at current prices. I'd ask for less, but given these levels of honesty it's hard to not lose money saving the world...

I expect that aidrop spammer guy will soon stop giving free $SUPRA for nothing and - given these lending losses - I think the not-for-profit lenders will also go away.

Only profiteers will remain in the game. The only way lending can always work.

The problem​

xx Network has a small, but growing number of nodes owned by centralized entities ("pools"). As far as I can tell most Substrate-based chains do.

I happen to care about xx Network and I don't think we should have anyone control more nodes than the current cMixx round size (which is five).

There are variuos measures the xx Network Council has initiated to protect enhance decentralization, but as a demonstration of a working decentralized approach I'm willing to spend some of my XX coins to make a point.

Did you know...​

This is only loosely related, but: did you know that Polkadot has a community event called "The Spammering" that is about to happen?

The Spammening, Polkadot’s ultimate stress test!

Polkadot is about to be put to the test as we push it to its limits in real-time. Get ready to witness the network’s resilience and scalability under extreme load as we #RekTheMeter!

This game can be thought of as a watered-down version of xx Network spammering (and in the opposite direction).

The Mission in xx Network era 1107​

Your mission is to block an IPv4 address of a cMix ID node (the cMix ID) during the game era ("the era", which is 1107).

That IPv4 address will belong to a pool node identified by the cMix ID.

The cMix ID will be shared 3-4 hours before the era begins so that you have time to find and block the IP (which takes mere seconds - see below).

note

If less than six valid applications are accepted for era 1107, the game will continue in era 1108 for the remaining slots.

Rewards​

Rewards for participating validators​

You will get over 100% of your normal validator reward if you successfully complete the mission. Those who partially (>50% failure rate) succeed will also get rewarded, but less.

Assumption:

  • You make 40 XX in most recent era directly preceding the game era(s) (era 1106)
    • Assumption: node earnings 182 XX x 22% (maximum validator commission acceptable for this game)

Examples of earnings for two outcomes with rewards:

  • Validator successfully fails 100% of cMixx rounds with the target during the game
    • Due to these failures you make 30 XX in the era (lose 10 XX, (40 - 30)). I will compensate you for the 10 XX
    • You will receive a separate bonus of 100% of your earnings from the previous era (40 XX * 100%)
    • My net payment to your address: 50 XX
  • Validator fails between 50%+1 and less than 100% of the rounds shared with the target
    • Due to less failures than the more successful players, drop in earnings is maybe just 5 XX (40-35)
    • I will compensate you for the 5 XX and pay 40% of the earnings from previous era: (40 - 35) + (40 x 40%)
    • My net payment to your address: 21 XX
  • Validator participates but is incompetent and doesnt' hit > 50% failure rate
    • They may lose 2-3 XX ("small success") or nothing (ineffective application of firewall rules)
    • They get no compensation and no reward, so if you can't take the risk just join the chat
    • I may compensate their nominators depending on the type and extent of screw-up (stupid screwups - no compensation)
      • Example of a stupid scrweup: you block IP address of the cMixx permissioning server instead of the cMixx ID you're supposed to

Separately, I will compensate all of your nominators for the difference (drop) in rewards for the era compared to the previous era.

Additional rewards may be dispensed depending on effectiveness of the game and if less than 10 validators participate in era 1107, the game will continue into era 1108 and I may increase rewards for the remaining slots in era 1108.

info

If you participate in era 1107 but less than 6 validators participate in that era: you may also apply to participate in era 1108.

Example: if 4 people apply in era 1107, we'll take 6 people in era 1108. Applications for era 1108 will be possible as soon as era 1107 begins because by then we'll know how many unused slots we have for era 1108.

Your earnings in era 1106 will be used in era 1108 reward formulas because the same validators may participate in both era 1107 and 1108.

If five or more validators hit 100% failure rate with the target(s), I may give additional rewards to those five validators. I may also lower the criteria for above rewards if the target makes frequent changes to the IP address that make achieving these objectives hard.

Please check the rules and qualifying criteria.

Technical tips​

How to find IP address for the validator?​

Search the cMixx log for cMix ID 0wjnYzegPz42ZZVc_dpB0RMRfddbEbdy_AkmsBbyrdMC or its substring:

cat /opt/xxnetwork/log/cmix.log | grep "Disconnected from 0wjnYzegPz42ZZVc"

How to block and unblock an IP address?​

sudo ufw deny from <ip address>
tip

Use sudo ufw status numbered before and after you add the rule, and note the new rule number.

You can delete this new rule later with sudo ufw delete NUMBER where NUMBER is the new rule(s) you added.

For more tips see the official examples at ubuntu.com.

What happens to my validator if I participate?​

Rounds in which the blocked IP participates would fail due to pre-comp timeout. Other rounds will work fine.

If your node was to fail 20% of the rounds, you'd make close to 20% less, i.e. your validator earnings would drop by almost 20% of your commission (assuming your commission is 22% that would be 22% - (20% x 22%) or a drop of less than 5 XX).

Your nominators will be similarly impacted, but they'll be fully compensated as I've explained above.

Questions and answers about the game​

What happens if the target changes their IP address?​

There are several potential targets, so they're unlikely to watch our Space and change IP address before or during our little game. If they do, though, let's consider some scenarios:

  • the target changes their IP address before beginning of the era: nothing changes for us. You may want to delete the outdated firewall rule and create a new one, and still earn partial rewards even if you do that hours into the game era - you just need to fail more than 50% of common rounds shared with the target
  • the target changes their IP address after beginning of the game era: my reward criteria will likely be loosened, but you can still modify your firewall rules to make more
    • if they change the node IP eight hours into an era and you update your rule 10 hours into an era, you can still meet the objective for more than 50% of the rounds and get the second tier reward
    • if they change several times during the game era (which I can check in my own logs), rewards will be prorated for the longest stretch (e.g. 6 hours) between two changes will be considered. In that case your node would make normal earnings for 24-6 hours (as blocking would fail), and I'd pay out a quarter (6/24 hours) of rewards. For six hours of successful blocking on a node that made 50 XX in that era, I'd reward you 50 x (1/4) x 100% (12.5 XX) if all of your rounds shared with the target during those six hours failed

In any case, you and your nominators won't make less in the era unless you act completely incompetently and mess up your node.

How can I time the firewall rule to begin or end just before or after the era?​

Use the sleep command or use OS scheduler. For example, you can login as root (or a passwordless sudoer) and run the rule delete command after the sleep command.

sudo ufw deny ...        # enable blocking 
sudo ufw list numbered   # note the rule number
su - root                # passwordless suders don't need this
sleep 87000 && ufw delete ... # remove the rule (by the number)

Or simply just login tomorrow and remove the rule any time in the next era.

How does the nominator compensation work?​

In the era before the game, your node earns 190 XX. In the era, if you do well your node will earn less, let's say 160 XX.

I will compensate your nominators for that difference (which would be (190 x 22%) - (160 x 22%)).

I will check every winner's nominators in the game era and send each nominator their ideal share of the loss. For example: if a nominator has 30% of all nominations, they'll get 30% of 6.6 XX (total drop of nominators' earnings after validator's commission is deducted).

What is the expected outcome​

It is to estimate the effect (reward reduction for bad actors and their nominators) and costs (drop in rewards for the rest of us) of a community action like this. It's a special take on Polkadot's spammering, so to speak.

Ideally I'd like the community to be able to hurt pools in a cost-effective fashion.

Alternatively, the network can use formal measures by the Council or democracy vote, but voluntary community action is vastly superior to that approach.

note

Ideally, it would be organized by the community and I wouldn't have to spend my time and tokens doing all this on my own, but it is what it is.

Is denying IPv4 access the best way​

It's not, but it's easiest.

Participants are free to use any way they want to fail rounds with the target.

Sample game workflow for validators​

Sign your validator wallet address using the same address as explained above.

Paste the address and signature into the Haven Space:

VALIDATOR
Address: <yourValidatorWalletAddress>
Signature: <generatedSignature>
tip

Haven v0.3.22 has a paste bug. Use CTRL+P or similar if pasting with the mouse does not work.

I'll review and - at least two hours before start of the era - publish up to 10 accepted participants' addresses and name the target (example: cMix ID 0wjnYzegPz42ZZVc_dpB0RMRfddbEbdy_AkmsBbyrdMC).

Find the IP address:

cat /opt/xxnetwork/log/cmix.log | grep "Disconnected from 0wjnYzegPz42ZZVc"

Let's say this shows you the IP address is 1.2.3.4.

Shortly before the era begins, block the IP and note the UFW rule number:

sudo ufw deny from 1.2.3.4
sudo sudo ufw list numbered
warning

Do not paste the example IP address (1.2.3.4). Use the correct target's IP address. You may create multiple rules if you wish.

Then record the new rule numbers - you'll need them to remove them later.

After the era begins, use the xx Network Dashboard to confirm rounds are falling.

You may login or recheck the dashboard during the era to confirm the target is still using the same IP. If not, remove the existing UFW rule and update it to continue blocking.

You can do this to remove the rule in little over 24 hours.

su - root
sleep 87000 && ufw delete <ruleNumber>

Do not log out. If that is not secure, then use screen to issue the command and detach, or create a one-time cronjob.

tip

87,000 seconds is 1 day and 10 minutes.

Use multiple delete commands to delete multiple rules.

For multiple rules, delete them in descending order. If you added rules 4, 5 and 6, first delete 6, then 5, then 4. Otherwise delete rule 4 three times.

Or you can login in the next era and remove the rule(s) with sudo ufw delete <ruleNumber>.

Login after the era to confirm the node is no longer blocked in ufw. Or keep them blocked, if you want to help the network.
